# Design Principles

### 1. Low-Pressure Interaction

The app should support casual, lightweight interactions without creating pressure to respond or perform.

_Why this matters:_
User research showed that people prefer low-effort communication (quick updates, passive viewing) 
and feel uncomfortable with performative features like likes, comments, or group chat expectations.

_How it translates into design:_

- No public comments or visible like counts
- Private replies instead of public interaction
- Passive engagement (people can view updates without needing to respond)
- Simple posting formats (text, photo, voice memo, prompts)

### 2. Intentional Connection

The app should encourage users to actively maintain friendships through small, meaningful actions.

_Why this matters:_
Academic research highlights that friendships require intentional effort and consistency, 
while user research showed that people often lose touch due to “out of sight, out of mind” dynamics.

_How it translates into design:_

- “On This Day” memory prompts to re-spark connection
- Gentle nudges to reach out or make plans
- Surfacing friends you haven’t interacted with recently
- Highlighting shared history between users

### 3. Flexible Communication Styles

The app should adapt to different ways people express and receive care, instead of enforcing one communication style.

_Why this matters:_
Users have very different preferences (in-person vs texting vs passive interaction), 
so a rigid system would not support all types of friendships.

_How it translates into design:_

- Multiple ways to interact (messages, updates, voice memos, plans)
- No required frequency of interaction
- Customizable visibility (who sees what)
- No pressure toward constant engagement

### 4. Reduce Planning Friction

Planning and coordination should be simple, intuitive, and require minimal effort.

_Why this matters:_
Planning was identified as one of the biggest barriers in user research, often leading to no follow-through.

_How it translates into design:_

- Integrated calendar + event system
- Easy invite / RSVP flows
- Clear visibility of upcoming plans
- Group-based coordination (orbits)

### 5. Relationship-Centered Design (not content-centered)

The app should prioritize relationships between people rather than content consumption.

_Why this matters:_
Existing tools focus on content (posts, feeds, algorithms), 
while both academic and user research show the need for tools that support relationships themselves.

_How it translates into design:_

- Feed organized around friends, not popularity
- No algorithmic ranking or viral mechanics
- Emphasis on small groups (orbits)
- Features tied to specific people (shared albums, memories)

### 6. Encourage Continuity Over Time

The app should help friendships feel like ongoing narratives rather than disconnected moments.

_Why this matters:_
Existing tools capture isolated memories, but don’t help build a sense of shared history or continuity.

_How it translates into design:_

- Shared photo albums tied to friendships/groups
- Memory resurfacing (On This Day)
- Visible history of interactions or plans
- Emphasis on long-term connection, not just recent activity

### 7. Emotional Safety and Privacy

Users should feel safe sharing without fear of judgment, comparison, or social pressure.

_Why this matters:_
User research showed discomfort with performative social media and fear of one-sided effort.

_How it translates into design:_

- No public metrics (likes, views, etc.)
- Controlled sharing (choose who sees each post)
- No exposure of “who interacts more”
- Private, one-on-one responses
