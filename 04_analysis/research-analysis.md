# Research Analysis

To have a better understanding of the problem space, I did background research, examining both academic studies and existing tools, 
and user research, by conducting interviews and then creating an empathy map based on those findings.
Looking at these together helped me move from general ideas about friendship to more concrete insights about what people actually need 
and where current tools fail.

### Connecting Background Research and User Research

Across both research areas, a consistent theme emerged:
friendships are highly valuable, but difficult to maintain in practice.

Academic research highlights the importance of friendships for mental and physical well-being, 
while also emphasizing that they require intentional effort and regular interaction to stay strong. 
At the same time, user interviews revealed that although people do care about maintaining friendships, 
they often struggle to do so due to everyday barriers like busy schedules, misaligned routines, and lack of coordination.

This creates a clear gap between intention and action. 
People want to be good friends, but do not always have the tools or systems to support that consistently.

### Gaps in Existing Tools

This gap is further reinforced when looking at existing digital tools.

Current platforms focus on isolated aspects of friendship:

- Messaging apps support communication but not relationship maintenance
- Planning tools support logistics but not emotional connection
- Photo-sharing apps capture memories but do not actively encourage reconnection
- Tracking tools introduce intentionality but feel transactional and impersonal

User research indirectly reflects these limitations, participants described: 

- Feeling overwhelmed by fragmented communication across platforms
- Avoiding social media due to its performative nature
- Struggling with planning due to effort and decision fatigue

Overall, no existing tool supports friendships in a interconnected, multidimensional, low-pressure, and meaningful way.

### Key Behavioral Insights

From the interviews, many important patterns help define what a more effective solution should consider: 

- **Low pressure interaction is essential:** people prefer casual, lightweight ways of staying in touch rather than structured or demanding modes of communication
- **Friendships are unique to each person:** different people have different communication styles, needs, and expectations, meaning flexibility is key
- **Planning is a major source of tension:** even when people want to meet, the effort required to coordinate often prevents it from happening
- **Closeness is not defined by frequency:** some friendships remain strong even with infrequent interaction, suggesting that tools should support different rhythms of connection.
- **Uncertainty can impede/block action:** fear of one-sided effort or not knowing where you stand in a friendship can stop people from reaching out

### Overall Insight

Combining all of these findings, the core issue is not that people lack friendships or care about them less.
Instead, the problem lies in the absence of tools that support intentional, low-pressure, and meaningful connection over time.

There is a clear opportunity to design a platform that:

- Bridges the gap between wanting to reach out and actually doing it
- Reduces friction in both communication and planning
- Supports ongoing connection, not just one-time interactions
- Moves away from performative social media toward something more personal and relationship-focused

This research confirms that maintaining friendships is both important and increasingly challenging, 
especially in the context of modern digital communication. 
While many tools exist, they remain fragmented and do not fully address the emotional and practical needs of sustaining relationships.
