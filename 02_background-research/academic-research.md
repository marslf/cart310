# Academic Research

## Psychological / Social Research

### [The science of why friendships keep us healthy](https://www.apa.org/monitor/2023/06/cover-story-science-friendship) 
#### By Zara Abrams, published by the American Psychological Association

- **Friendships protect mental and physical health:** 
People with supportive friends report higher life satisfaction and lower risk of depression, heart problems, 
and premature death (Choi et al., 2020; Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010; Steptoe et al., 2013).

- **High-quality friendships matter:** 
Close, supportive friendships provide a buffer against stress and improve emotional regulation. 
Minimal interactions with friends or acquaintances can also positively impact well-being.

- **Fiendship skills:**
Friendships teach empathy, support-seeking, and identity development (which also strengthen romantic relationships later).

- **Friendships need intentionality:**
Without regular interactions, relationships can weaken. 
Young adult friendships may struggle due to conflicting needs, misunderstandings, or difficulties providing support.

- **Social isolation is a growing problem:** 
Loneliness and poor-quality social connections are increasing in modern society, amplified by technology and the pandemic. 
This highlights a need for tools to support maintaining friendships.  

_**Implication for project:**_  
These findings justify designing a tool that helps people intentionally maintain friendships, through more than just planning events, 
but also by supporting ongoing, meaningful connection and engagement with friends.
It proves the real positive impact that this tool could have if it became a reality.

### [The Friendship Recession: The Lost Art of Connecting](https://www.happiness.hks.harvard.edu/february-2025-issue/the-friendship-recession-the-lost-art-of-connecting)
#### By Carolyn Bruckmann, published by the Cneter for Public Leadership of Harvard Kennedy School

- **Declining friendships:** 
Since 1990, Americans with no close friends quadrupled; social time has dropped significantly (Bruckmann, 2025).

- **Digital interactions aren’t enough:** 
Online friendships cannot fully replace in-person interactions; physical presence triggers biological bonding responses.

- **Intentionality matters:** 
Forming friendships requires effort: planning shared experiences, embracing discomfort, and showing up.

- **Rituals and consistency sustain friendships:** 
Regular meetups, recurring group activities, and shared challenges deepen bonds.

- **Cultural barriers:** 
Work, family responsibilities, and declining community infrastructure reduce natural social opportunities.

**Implication for project:** 
An app that helps users intentionally form and sustain friendships, through organizing shared experiences 
and creating repeatable social rituals, could counteract cultural trends contributing to social isolation.
